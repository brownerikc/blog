I don't know how it happened, but the Dungeon Crawler Carl series is having a moment. I first heard about it from some coworkers who were reading it and excited about the impending release of the new book. Next I hear it's being adapted into a TV series, and all of a sudden almost everyone I know who's reading anything is getting into it. So it seemed like the easiest recommendation in the world, and something to add to my Saturday morning reading queue.

Well, I'm about halfway through the first book. And man, this just isn't working for me. I'm still going to finish it and maybe it'll finish strong, but I've already been mentally inventorying the problems I'm having.

Here's a short list of my impressions:

## Too much premise, not enough story

I think the underlying premise is perfectly good. A Hunger Games-esque competition in the framing of Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy is a very workable idea. But a premise is just a starting point. You need to tell a story with it. This involves characters encountering interesting conflicts and dealing with them in compelling ways. So far, the conflicts are largely "There are monsters and bosses that want to kill me, so I must kill them." That's boring, what even is the point of that?

And the thing is, I feel like there's already something there to work with: Donut the cat. This is a character who has known Carl for a long time and now that she's able to speak, she has plenty of observations about Carl that could provide a natural source of conflict. This could be a way to interrogate Carl's character, but so far there's just way too little of that.

## It should have started in media res

The book opens with too much setup. The first chapter contains what amounts to an expositional tutorial, describing the dungeon and its rules. Then the next few chapters are about Carl reaching the character tutorial (which, again, is basically saying "this all works like a video game"). Aside from a brief goblin encounter, it's all explanation and no action.

Stories don't need that much setup. You just need to get the reader intrigued somehow. There are many ways you can do this, and the premise provides a very natural option: present a dungeon encounter to start with some action, then have something using that video game logic happen. (I don't know, pulling out a healing potion from Carl's inventory or something?) This is disorienting, but in a way that gets the reader to think "Wait, how did we get here and why does it work like this?" From there, you can flash back to the chronological beginning to fill in the answers because you've primed the reader to actually care about that expositional dump.

I don't think this is definitively how the story should have started. But the beginning just really didn't hook me. It needed to do something different.

## Worn-out snark

Item descriptions and achievements pop up frequently, presumably written by the dungeon masters. They're filled with quippy snark that seems to be the norm for stories in geek and video game culture these days. And man, I'm just tired of this. It's rarely ever funny anymore, and it just feels like it's the writer trying to signal they're too cool to care.

## Books are not video games

The writing feels like it's too concerned with making everything sound like a video game. Crawlers have a menu-based UI that they can somehow pull up within their own vision and mentally play around with. Inventories are some mysterious dimension things go to, accessible at will. A lot of words are spent describing all this happening. But to what end?

None of this contributes meaningfully to the storytelling. The closest it comes is by having level ups indicate character growth. But "number goes up" may be the worst way of writing growth I've ever seen. Aside from that, all this style feels like it's saying is "wouldn't it be fun if video game UIs were real?"

I'm certainly open to the idea that there could be something narratively interesting to do with this. If The Lego Movie could find something meaningful to say about toys, surely someone could do the same for video games. But as it is, this style just reads like someone is narrating themselves playing a video game.

## I fundamentally do not care

Ultimately, all my observations are specific critiques that boil down to this. Good storytelling makes the reader care about what's happening or the characters involved, and I just don't. The characters just haven't shown themselves to be interesting, the actual events are not compelling writing, and the framing is annoying.

I'm going to finish the book. It's not fundamentally unreadable, and I believe there's value in experiencing media you don't like. But unless this book turns everything around in the second half, I can't see myself continuing with the rest of the series.

## Coda: bizarre objectification

There's one other problem I have that doesn't fit into "I don't care" like everything else, but it's significant enough to mention: there's some really weird objectification going on that's hard to ignore.

Early on, there's a corpse of a woman that Carl notes has been stripped of clothes and that there's a gunshot wound near her breast. It felt a little gratuitous in the moment, but something I could give a pass.

Later, he meets another woman and observes that she has a badge that is hanging over her breast. This is getting weirder, why do you need to be that specific?

The chapter I just finished features a talk show host and Carl spends multiple paragraphs focused on her breasts.

What are we even doing here? I'm going to hope this is meant to be some sort of characterization of Carl that wildly misfired. But I also get the feeling that this isn't the author's intent.
